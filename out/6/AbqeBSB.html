Dustin Moskovitz was invited
Works by: Yerin Kim, Maria Guzman Capron, and Daniel Chen Curated by: Josh Stulen Opening Reception Saturday, August 2nd, 6:00-11:00 PM Light Refreshments will be served, and Mark Slee will be DJing. Vicci Jang and Lex Kosieradzki will be playing cello and guitar. Closing party Sunday, August 3rd, 5:00-8:00 PM http:// heronarts.com “Wallflowers … those shy ones! Oh! Cling! Cling thee to the furthermost borders—the hinterlands. Sublimate, oh, sublimate thy libidinal impulses into decorative organic motifs … ornamental hair growths, aesthetically placed tattoos. Beauty-mark thyself! … Oh! Oh! Oh!” – Mike Kelley Wallflowers stand on the edges, serving as an emotional receptacle for the lives of others. Their ability to slow things down and analyze situations beyond the surface is often dismissed as anti-social behavior. This keen ability to see the underlying tension within an encounter is an acute skill that should not be ignored. Wallflowers are also quirky, unique individuals with their own personal aesthetic. They try to hide, but stand out because they are different. Wearing a camouflage of sorts, they blend into their environment to avoid being noticed, introverting so they can eavesdrop on the conversation from afar. If these walls––if these wallflowers––could only talk. When an artist makes a piece of art, it is essentially a way of voicing an opinion that cannot necessarily be expressed through words. The work stands in for the artist and serves as a representation of an experience that is personal––but, because art is an arbitrary field this experience gets abstracted and evolves into new interpretations via the viewer. The work enters an area that is beyond its concept or objecthood, as it becomes a part of a larger conversation within issues of feminism, escapism, anxiety, failure, fantasy, community, and stereotypes. The viewer carries this conversation with them out into society to share with others––the dialogue trickles down to expand beyond the art world. - Josh Stulen

His taste is rich and vast and he has the unique ability to create deeply rhythmic narratives that offer unexpected and exotic spices. He mastered his deep mixing skills with a cassette deck and a belt drive turntable in his father's den in Troy, home of contemporaries such as Mike Servito, Tadd Mullinex and Matt Dear. Equally entrenched in indie rock, post punk, experimental music, ambient, rock, house, and techno, Carlos was always a man of many worlds - with an ability to traverse though them with an unique imagination. There is a mood to his search within sound, something that is heavy, deep yet gritty. Like many Caribbean transplants that ends up in northern colder climates, he wonders where our rhythmic complexity is. For him it is effortless and endlessly surprising to the listener. Carlos is an important component of the Interdimensional Transmissions parties, bringing a loose yet tight moody deepness and an ever unexpected edge. At the original No Way Back party, he demanded truth in advertising, pushing us to and then past the 12 hour mark. He has participated in the studio with Ectomorph, and also performed alongside some of their live performances.
